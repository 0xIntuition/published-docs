Triples

Triples are constructs that combine three Atoms to form a relationship or assertion. They follow a simple Predicate-Subject-Object format. This format is easy for people to understand, and is austere enough to enable logical reasoning and inference for a variety of computer programs.

Triples connect three Atoms to express a fact or relationship between entities. Triples comprise of many similarities to Atoms in that they are indexed the same way (Atom ID vs Triple ID) and contain numerous Vaults with various Bonding Curves. An ID which can refer to either an Atom or a Triple is called a Term ID. In other words, a Term could be something like “Bob”, or a Term could be something like “Bob Is Friend.” But “Bob” will always be an Atom, and “Bob Is Friend” will always be a Triple.

However, instead of containing a URI like an Atom does, Triples contain a set of 3 Term IDs. The most general use case would be 3 Atom IDs, as in “Bob Is Friend.” Let’s say we have Atom IDs for “Bob” [32], “Is” [4], and “Friend” [409].

SubjectPredicateObject
Bob [32]Is [4]Friend [409]

This Triple might have the ID of 1,010,321.

A more complex use case, using nested Triples, might look like “[Link To Twitter Post] Disproves ‘Bob Is Friend’”

SubjectPredicateObject
x.com/post/938492 [8,407]Disproves [19]Bob Is Friend [1,010,321]

The beauty if this system is that it maintains a record of things which have been stated, yet allows information to be added over time which refines the “signal” and facilitates the refinement of truth.

One other thing Triples have which Atoms do not is called Counter Triples. While a statement like “Bob Is Friend” can have a negative, a singular concept like “Bob” cannot. There is no such thing as “Not Bob”. In order to keep things more organized in the Intuition System, we prefer to use Counter Triples to keep negative signal closely associated with its corresponding positive signal. So Triples have both positive and negative vaults, but Atoms only have vaults.
